Dwarf galaxies

Dwarf galaxies are small galaxies that contain far fewer stars than larger galaxies, such as spiral or elliptical galaxies. They come in different shapes and are often found orbiting larger galaxies. Even though they are much smaller, dwarf galaxies are still held together by gravity and can contain stars, gas, dust, and dark matter.

Dwarf galaxies are much smaller than most other galaxies, usually measuring only a few thousand light-years across. They can contain anywhere from a few million to several billion stars, compared with the hundreds of billions of stars found in larger galaxies. Many dwarf galaxies formed early in the universe and are thought to be the building blocks of larger galaxies.

One well-known example is the Large Magellanic Cloud, which orbits the Milky Way and is visible from the Southern Hemisphere. Another example is the Small Magellanic Cloud. They are called "dwarf galaxies" because they are much smaller and less massive than galaxies like the Milky Way. By studying dwarf galaxies, astronomers can learn more about how galaxies formed and evolved over billions of years.
